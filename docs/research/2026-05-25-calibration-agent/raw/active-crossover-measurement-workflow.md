# Active DSP commissioning for a DIY two-way smart speaker with Raspberry Pi 5 and CamillaDSP

## Technical summary

For JTS, the speaker-baseline layer should be treated as an electroacoustic commissioning problem, not as a room-EQ problem. The highest-confidence workflow is: measure each driver in the final enclosure and baffle with complex data, build the crossover and per-driver EQ from those measurements, align the drivers in time and polarity, validate the summed response both on-axis and off-axis, then freeze that baseline before adding any room correction or preference tilt. REW, VituixCAD, and the Hughes/Voice Coil workflow all converge on the same core requirement: meaningful crossover design needs magnitude **and** phase, common timing, and off-axis data, not just a pretty on-axis trace. citeturn23view0turn11view0turn6view3turn34view0

Among classic active crossover targets, an **acoustic Linkwitz-Riley fourth order** target remains the safest default for a conventional two-way. Rane’s primer describes LR4 as the de facto pro-audio standard, with in-phase outputs, 24 dB/octave slopes, and a summed acoustic response that is unity at crossover because each branch is at -6 dB there. In practice, that gives a forgiving baseline for woofer/tweeter integration, good driver protection relative to shallower slopes, and straightforward validation with summed-response and reverse-polarity checks. citeturn6view5

Butterworth and Bessel alignments are still legitimate tools, but they should be viewed as **measured alternatives**, not generic defaults. Butterworth families can be useful, but their electrical behavior does not automatically give the same in-phase acoustic summation behavior as LR4. Bessel families are generally discussed as more time-domain-oriented and gentler in roll-off, but that gentler roll-off reduces out-of-band attenuation and pushes more burden onto driver linearity and power handling. For a DIY smart speaker that values repeatability, safety, and easy validation, LR4 is usually where you start; you switch only if measurements show a better acoustic-plus-polar result with your actual drivers and spacing. citeturn6view5turn37search0turn37search3turn37search15

Crossover frequency is not chosen by one rule. It is constrained simultaneously by woofer directivity and breakup, tweeter distortion/excursion/power handling, center-to-center spacing and resulting vertical lobing, and the desired listening window. Linkwitz emphasized mounting drivers as close together as possible in a vertical line and correcting for mounting delay; Honeycutt emphasized avoiding abrupt directivity changes at crossover; Hughes showed that a crossover can look fine on-axis while creating severe vertical “holes” off-axis. The practical consequence is that the “right” crossover point is the one that best balances **directivity match, driver safety, and summed behavior**, not the one that looks best on a single on-axis SPL plot. citeturn14view3turn35view0turn34view0

Driver alignment must be proven acoustically. The relevant quantities are not only physical offset and baffle geometry, but also frequency-dependent acoustic center behavior, crossover phase rotation, and group delay. Hughes’ measurement guidance notes that the acoustic center can move with frequency and that complex measured phase, not Hilbert-derived phase, is required when simulation depends on summation. REW’s alignment tool and VituixCAD’s time-align function both formalize the same idea: use common timing, compare phase/group-delay behavior around crossover, and confirm the chosen delay/polarity by the quality of the summed response and the depth of the reverse-polarity null. citeturn23view0turn11view0turn9view0turn9view1

For JTS specifically, CamillaDSP maps cleanly to this layered view. It supports channel mixers, IIR biquads and FIR convolution, gain, polarity, delay, a clip-style limiter filter, and channel-aware compressor processors. It also explicitly supports active crossovers, REW filter export, and a room-correction convolution stage, which means JTS can keep loudspeaker-baseline commissioning, room correction, and user-preference tilt as separate named steps instead of one opaque “master correction” blob. citeturn17search0turn18view0turn18view1turn19view0turn30view0turn31view0

An important engineering note for JTS is that **IIR-first is the right baseline ladder**. CamillaDSP’s docs describe IIR as biquad-based and FIR as convolution-based, and note that long FIR filters raise chunk-size, efficiency, CPU, and latency tradeoffs. CamillaDSP also documents that even fairly modest hardware is intended for crossover and room-correction use, and its own examples show Raspberry Pi–class deployments and very large FIR workloads on a Pi 4. For a four-channel two-way active speaker, that means there is no architectural reason to start with a monolithic FIR crossover; begin with explicit IIR blocks and add FIR later only where its extra control is useful. citeturn19view2turn22search0turn17search0turn30view0

## Implementation ladder

**Bring-up stage.** Start with a four-output CamillaDSP configuration that does nothing except capture stereo, expand to four channels, and route left/right to woofer/tweeter outputs with all gains conservative and all room-correction steps disabled. CamillaDSP’s mixer model is built for exactly this pattern: route stereo to four outputs, then apply per-channel filter steps. For the tweeter outputs, include a temporary protective high-pass and conservative gain padding from the very first power-up. If the amplifiers connect directly to the tweeters, a series protection capacitor is strongly recommended in active systems to reduce risk from DC or turn-on/off transients. citeturn19view1turn19view3turn30view0turn26search16turn26search18turn26search23

**Raw driver measurement stage.** Measure the woofer and tweeter separately in the final cabinet/baffle, from the same mic position, with a common timing reference. REW’s acoustic timing reference creates common relative timing across measurements from the same mic position, which is exactly what crossover arithmetic and alignment require. Hughes also recommends measuring each passband separately where possible, with no crossover filters in place, because that captures the natural response of the drivers in the actual enclosure. If low-frequency far-field data are weak or reflection-limited, merge near-field and far-field data before simulation; VituixCAD explicitly supports this merged workflow. citeturn6view1turn23view0turn8view1turn6view3

**Directivity capture stage.** Do not stop at one axial measurement. Capture at least a workable horizontal and vertical set around the reference axis, with angular spacing fine enough to reveal lobes and nulls. Hughes’ modeling-file article warns that the angular resolution has to be finer than the loudspeaker’s lobes/nulls or the data will alias spatially, and VituixCAD is built around organized off-axis datasets, CTA-2034-style listening-window and sound-power views, and directivity charts. For a JTS-style guided workflow, even a limited off-axis set such as 0°, ±10°, ±20°, ±30° vertically and horizontally is dramatically better than none, though denser coverage is preferred if the rig allows it. citeturn23view0turn8view2turn8view3turn8view4

**Initial crossover synthesis stage.** Start with an acoustic LR4 target, not because it is “correct” in the abstract, but because it is stable, well understood, and easy to audit. Use per-driver EQ only to make each driver behave sensibly in its intended band and in some margin beyond the crossover, then implement delay, polarity, and gain trims. Honeycutt’s driver-selection guidance is useful here: the flatter the native driver behavior around crossover, the easier the crossover job becomes, and directivity transitions should be as smooth as possible rather than abrupt. After the first fit, inspect not only the axial sum but also the vertical directivity and listening-window behavior through crossover. citeturn6view5turn35view0turn34view0

**Time-alignment and null-validation stage.** Use REW’s alignment tool or VituixCAD’s time-align function to estimate delay, but treat that output as a hypothesis until it is validated by acoustic behavior. REW can align phase slopes or phase itself around the alignment frequency and may reveal that inversion is required; VituixCAD’s time-align workflow explicitly solves for the delay that best matches the measured sum. A strong reverse-polarity null through the crossover region is the most useful quick check that the two branches are truly meeting as intended at the measurement axis. Linkwitz described essentially the same process in his own design procedure: reverse polarity and adjust delay/phase for maximum cancellation at the design point. citeturn11view0turn9view0turn9view2turn12search17

**Freeze-baseline stage.** Once the active crossover, per-driver EQ, gain trims, polarity, and delay are behaving properly, freeze that as the loudspeaker-baseline preset. Only then add room correction. CamillaDSP’s room-correction example is explicitly a separate convolution workflow, and REW’s guidance on EQ limits is a strong reason not to let seat-specific room compensation leak backward into loudspeaker commissioning. In JTS, the baseline preset should remain independently bypassable forever. citeturn31view0turn28view0

## Recommended safe defaults

A conservative default for a general two-way JTS loudspeaker is an **acoustic LR4 crossover**, implemented initially with IIR biquads rather than FIR. That gives steep enough slopes for practical woofer/tweeter protection and straightforward interpretation of polarity and summed-response behavior. FIR only becomes the better choice when you specifically need linear-phase crossover behavior, phase correction, or convolution-based correction kernels that are difficult to express with IIR blocks alone. citeturn6view5turn17search0turn22search3

Use **measured complex data** as the source of truth. Make the reference axis explicit, keep the mic position fixed for driver-integration work, and always use a timing reference. If a measurement lacks stable phase or stable timing, it is fine for rough magnitude viewing but not for final crossover alignment. For JTS, that means the guided tool should plainly mark a measurement as “magnitude-only / unsafe for alignment” when timing is absent or suspect. citeturn6view1turn11view0turn23view0turn27search6

For the tweeter, safe defaults should assume **hardware mistakes will happen**. Use a temporary or permanent series protection capacitor in active mode, start with an additional DSP high-pass that is more conservative than the eventual target, and only reduce that conservatism after the loudspeaker is measured and stable. MiniDSP’s active-speaker notes recommend a protection capacitor in series with each tweeter in direct-drive active systems, and Klippel’s protection documentation shows the broader protection logic of keeping low-frequency energy away from fragile HF devices. citeturn26search16turn26search18turn26search21turn26search23turn25search0turn25search9

For levels, begin with **low sweep level, long sweep, and a tweeter-first safety mindset**. REW recommends single longer sweeps rather than multiple shorter sweeps for best results; ARTA explicitly tells the user to monitor input levels and stop if any input clips. In a guided JTS workflow, the software should require confirmation that the tweeter path is protected before enabling any isolated-tweeter sweep that reaches below the final acoustic crossover region. citeturn10search4turn32search1

In CamillaDSP, use the following logical order as a default: routing/mixing, per-driver crossover, per-driver EQ, per-driver delay/polarity/gain, per-driver protection, then optional room/preference stages as distinct named blocks. Keep room correction and preference tilt outside the speaker-baseline preset, even if they use the same underlying FIR engine. CamillaDSP’s own examples and docs support this style of layered configuration, with separate mixers, filter steps, processors, and convolution stages. citeturn19view2turn19view4turn30view0turn31view0

For dynamics protection, treat CamillaDSP’s built-in **Limiter** as a final clip stop, not as the only driver-safety mechanism. The docs describe it as hard or soft clipping, while the compressor processor supplies attack, release, threshold, factor, and optional clip limit. That is useful, but it is not the same thing as a dedicated look-ahead loudspeaker-management limiter. The safe default is therefore: conservative gain structure first, crossover and HF protection second, compressor third, clip limit last. citeturn18view0turn18view1turn18view2

## Visualizations for a guided tool

The minimum useful visualization set is not just “before/after EQ.” JTS should show **individual woofer and tweeter magnitude and phase**, the **summed response**, and the **reverse-polarity summed response** around crossover. REW and VituixCAD both make clear that alignment decisions without phase-aware summation are unreliable, and Hughes’ directivity work shows that an on-axis-only view can miss severe off-axis crossover failures. A reverse-polarity null plot is especially valuable because it turns a subtle phase/alignment problem into an obvious success/failure signal. citeturn11view0turn10search2turn9view0turn34view0

The next layer is **time-domain verification**: impulse response, step response if available, relative arrival time, and group delay. REW exposes impulse, phase, and group-delay views, while VituixCAD explicitly shows normal group delay, phase, and time-domain behavior of the simulated result. These views are important because two crossover solutions can look similar in smoothed magnitude but differ materially in delay behavior or impulse alignment. citeturn10search2turn1search18turn9view1turn8view0

A serious commissioning tool also needs **directivity-aware views**. At minimum, show vertical off-axis overlays such as 0°, ±10°, ±20°, ±30° through the crossover region. Better still, show a vertical directivity map or beamwidth trace, because Hughes demonstrates that crossover tweaks can leave the on-axis sum nearly unchanged while materially improving or degrading the vertical radiation pattern. VituixCAD’s CTA-2034, sound-power, DI, and directivity-chart views are strong models for what the mature version of JTS should eventually emulate. citeturn34view0turn8view2turn8view3turn8view4

For automation, REW’s local API is relevant prior art. It exposes endpoints for measurements and even the alignment tool, including aligned sums and filtered impulse responses. That makes a semi-automated JTS workflow realistic: a user could take measurements in REW, have JTS fetch the data, run checks for timing validity and null depth, and then emit CamillaDSP YAML or a preset patch rather than trying to reinvent the entire measurement front end. citeturn10search3turn19view0

## Failure modes and guardrails

The biggest failure mode is **optimizing the on-axis trace while ruining the vertical listening window**. Hughes’ crossover-directivity article is the cleanest warning: a system may sum well on-axis but develop major off-axis holes and narrowed beamwidth in the crossover region. The guardrail is mandatory off-axis validation before a preset is accepted as “commissioned.” JTS should refuse to label a crossover final if only one axial trace exists. citeturn34view0

Another common failure mode is **bogus phase caused by bad timing practice**. REW is explicit that arithmetic and alignment with phase-bearing traces require proper timing alignment, and its acoustic timing reference exists specifically to create shared timing between measurements taken from the same mic position. JTS should therefore surface timing provenance in the UI and warn when a measurement was taken without loopback or acoustic reference. citeturn6view1turn11view0

A third failure mode is **using room-seat measurements to solve loudspeaker design problems**. REW’s EQ guidance is blunt: EQ is position-specific, cannot fix narrow deep dips, and cannot repair non-minimum-phase room behavior or late reflections. CamillaDSP’s room-correction example likewise states that correction is tied to one microphone position and does not fix late reflections. The guardrail is architectural separation: JTS should maintain distinct speaker-baseline, room-correction, and preference presets, each bypassable on its own. citeturn28view0turn31view0

A fourth failure mode is **over-trusting DSP protection and under-valuing physical protection**. CamillaDSP’s limiter is clip-based, not a full loudspeaker-management ecosystem. MiniDSP’s active-speaker guidance strongly recommends a series protection capacitor for tweeters when amplifiers are connected directly to drivers. JTS should assume that muting bugs, wake/sleep pops, and config mistakes are possible, and should recommend a hardware protection capacitor unless the amplifier chain already guarantees DC-safe, pop-free behavior. citeturn18view0turn18view1turn26search16turn26search18turn26search23

A fifth failure mode is **treating acoustic-center offset as a fixed geometric constant in every direction**. VituixCAD’s help is careful here: delay/distance differences between acoustic centers are not constant across all directions for all radiator/enclosure types, so simple time-alignment workflows primarily support on-axis and listening-window simulation. The guardrail is humility: use delay as a commissioning control, but validate it on vertical off-axis traces rather than assuming the same delay “solves” all radiation directions. citeturn9view0turn9view1

A final failure mode is **creating a CamillaDSP config that is logically correct but operationally fragile**. CamillaDSP warns that bypassing a mixer that changes channel count can break the pipeline, and long FIR filters require chunk-size/latency/CPU tradeoffs. For JTS, that means the generated config should have stable channel labels, minimally bypassable routing, and a performance budget check before enabling long convolution presets. citeturn19view2turn19view3turn22search0

## Open questions and limitations

This research did **not** include a specific woofer/tweeter pair, enclosure alignment, baffle geometry, target SPL, listening distance, or amplifier headroom budget. Because of that, the exact crossover frequency, final slope, EQ shape, compressor threshold, and clip limit cannot be finalized here; they must be derived from the actual driver measurements and intended SPL target. citeturn23view0turn35view0

The open literature reviewed here is much stronger on **LR4 practice, measurement workflow, directivity, and alignment** than it is on modern comparative practice for Butterworth versus Bessel in contemporary DIY active two-ways. That is why the recommendation here is measurement-first and LR4-first, not topology-agnostic. citeturn6view5turn37search0turn37search3

CamillaDSP’s built-in protection building blocks are useful, but the documentation reviewed here describes clipping and compressor-style dynamics, not a full look-ahead loudspeaker-management limiter. For fragile tweeters, JTS should therefore continue to assume that **hardware protection and conservative gain structure** remain necessary even after DSP protection is enabled. citeturn18view0turn18view1turn26search23
