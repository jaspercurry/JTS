# Fact-Check: Driver Linearization Claims for an Active DSP Crossover System

> Verbatim owner-commissioned deep-research artifact (2026-07-23) —
> claim-by-claim verification of the engineering spec (02) with primary
> sources. See this directory's README for adoption notes.

## TL;DR
- **Most of the technical claims hold up, but the "flat-to-20 kHz" framing needs three corrections:** KEF and Adam achieve their extended flat response primarily by **transducer design plus a fixed factory filter**, not by field calibration; and a B&C DE250 on a horn can be brought close to flat to ~18 kHz with magnitude EQ, but its native rolloff and phase-plug interference above ~10–14 kHz set a ceiling that magnitude EQ cannot fully invert.
- **The pivotal minimum-phase claim (E) is CONFIRMED with caveats:** a single driver's on-axis response is largely minimum-phase in its passband (so magnitude EQ is invertible there), but breakup, crossover summation, ports, diffraction, and multi-source phase-plug interference are the documented exceptions where excess phase appears — the software's excess-phase criterion is sound in principle but fragile in practice (it needs good SNR and accurate bulk-delay removal).
- **The single most actionable correction:** verifying **in-room** response against a FLAT target is wrong — the preferred in-room steady-state curve slopes DOWN ~1 dB/octave above the transition (Olive/Harman) — but flat IS correct for **gated quasi-anechoic per-driver** verification. Keep those two verification layers distinct.

## Details

### A. KEF Uni-Q, tangerine waveguide, and "flat to 20 kHz"
KEF's own material confirms the Uni-Q tweeter is a **25 mm (1") vented aluminium dome** mounted at the acoustic centre of the mid/bass cone. The **Tangerine Waveguide** is a real radial-channel structure that KEF describes as creating "a slight compression loading effect resulting in slightly higher output gain," with the extra gain "seen from around 7 kHz to around 15 kHz." Quantified more precisely, KEF's Jack Oclee-Brown has stated the waveguide "provides gain for the tweeter up to a maximum of about 3.5 dB at 15 kHz, and helps subdue the natural breakup mode of the tweeter's dome." So KEF explicitly uses a passive/mechanical waveguide to shape HF and to damp breakup — flatness here is engineered into the transducer, not corrected in the field.

**Metamaterial Absorption Technology (MAT) targets the REAR wave** behind the dome — a labyrinth that absorbs the backwave to reduce a resonance that would otherwise reflect through the dome. It is NOT the mechanism for extending on-axis HF flatness to 20 kHz. If the user's model conflated MAT with top-octave on-axis flattening, that should be corrected: MAT reduces coloration/distortion from the rear cavity, not the top-octave on-axis shape.

Notably, for the LS50 Meta Uni-Q specifically, KEF states the aluminium dome's **natural breakup mode occurs at about 40 kHz** — considerably higher than the generic 20–28 kHz figure for typical 1" aluminium domes, because KEF's dome geometry and vented design push it well out of band. This is the answer to the user's core "documented breakup frequency" question for KEF: ~40 kHz for the Uni-Q dome.

Measured data confirm "flat-looking to 20 kHz" as a RESULT of transducer + fixed crossover, not field DSP. Stereophile's John Atkinson measured the LS50 Meta's farfield (30° horizontal average) as "superbly even from the lower midrange through the treble," with "the tweeter's output remain[ing] at full level almost to the 20 kHz limit," and no mid-treble discontinuities from cone-edge diffraction. Erin's Audio Corner / spinorama Klippel NFS data show a mean SPL ~83 dB and a smooth on-axis response to well above 20 kHz. A passive LS50 Meta has no per-unit field calibration — so KEF's answer to "transducer, EQ, or both?" is **both transducer design and a fixed passive crossover voicing.**

### B. Adam X-ART / AMT
Adam's X-ART is an Air Motion Transformer (Heil principle): a pleated diaphragm that squeezes air from its folds with a velocity transform ratio Adam publishes as **4:1** (confirmed verbatim on adam-audio.com spec sheets, alongside equivalent diaphragm Ø 2"/56 mm and area 2420 mm²). The published **diaphragm weight is 0.17 g (170 mg)** — so **"tens of milligrams" is wrong by roughly an order of magnitude.** Frequency response is specified to **50 kHz** (Sound on Sound gives the −3 dB point at 50 kHz for the A7X), comfortably past 30 kHz — so "genuinely flat past 30 kHz" is CONFIRMED as a manufacturer spec (third-party measurements typically show gentle ripple, not ruler-flat). The T-series uses a different, less costly AMT than the S/A-series. Oskar Heil's original AMT patent describes the folded-diaphragm velocity-transformation principle. Adam applies DSP correction in its DSP-equipped models (e.g., A-series "V", S-series), so factory/onboard DSP voicing contributes to the flat spec in those models.

### C. Design-time vs field calibration
This distinction is well-supported. The **Klippel Near Field Scanner** publishes measurement accuracy of **±0.1 dB in the maximum-SPL (on-axis) direction and ±1 dB in all directions**, over 10 Hz–20 kHz (with the Direct Sound Separation module, even in non-anechoic rooms). That is a lab-grade uncertainty no field USB-mic calibration approaches. Manufacturers linearize at design time against such data, producing a FIXED filter.

Field/room products deliberately do less at HF and concentrate at LF:
- **Genelec** monitors are "calibrated flat in anechoic free field conditions," and GLM AutoCal "applies relevant compensation in the low and low-mid frequencies to minimise the detrimental room acoustic anomalies," plus level/delay/subwoofer-phase alignment. Genelec's 8341A publishes an anechoic accuracy of **±1.5 dB (45 Hz–20 kHz)**, with −6 dB points at 38 Hz and 37 kHz.
- **Neumann** KH-line publishes tight factory anechoic specs. The KH 120 II: **±3 dB (44 Hz–21 kHz)** headline; **±1.25 dB pass-band (48 Hz–20.5 kHz)**; **±0.7 dB free-field linearity (100 Hz–10 kHz)**; per-unit reproduction accuracy of ±0.17/±0.30/±0.45 dB for 50/80/100% of units; and a stated unit tolerance of "no more than ±0.5 dB." Its MA-1 room calibration emphasizes room-mode/LF compensation and subwoofer summation.

So the framing is correct: design-time linearization has a far better HF ceiling than field calibration, and the manufacturers' own room tools deliberately do less at HF — consistent with Toole's argument that single-point HF room correction is unreliable. (Caveat: Neumann's public copy does not contain an explicit "each unit hand-aligned in an anechoic chamber" sentence; the tight ±0.5 dB tolerance and per-unit reproduction-accuracy stats imply rigorous per-unit measurement but are not phrased as individual calibration.)

### D. Compression-driver HF rolloff and phase-plug interference
CONFIRMED and grounded in primary literature. **Bob H. Smith, "An Investigation of the Air Chamber of Horn Type Loudspeakers," JASA 25(2):305–312, March 1953** (DOI 10.1121/1.1907038) is the foundational analysis: the horn throat's radial perturbation excites higher-order modes in the compression chamber, and "at the resonant frequencies of these modes the horn throat pressure becomes zero and the loudspeaker does not radiate." Smith showed how to place annular slots at modal nodes so that "each annulus does excite the first mode, but the excitation by the second annulus is out of phase with that of the first annulus… complete cancellation of the first mode results." This is explicitly path-length/geometric interference between multiple exits summing at the throat. **Mark Dodd & Jack Oclee-Brown, "A New Methodology for the Acoustic Design of Compression Driver Phase-Plugs with Concentric Annular Channels," JAES 57(10):771–787 (2009)** (AES preprint 7258, 123rd Convention, 2007) extends this and notes real diaphragm motion also excites resonances. **Earl Geddes'** work adds higher-order modes (HOM) in the waveguide/horn as a further HF irregularity mechanism.

On minimum phase: the individual transducer's axial response is generally minimum-phase (prosoundtraining measurement: "All devices were minimum phase, as is typical for individual transducers"). But the multi-exit phase-plug summation and horn HOMs are multi-source interference — the same category as the documented non-minimum-phase exceptions in E. The honest position: the smooth part of a compression driver's rolloff is minimum-phase and EQ-invertible; the interference-driven ripple near/above 10–14 kHz may contain non-minimum-phase components and is not fully invertible by magnitude EQ. **B&C DE250 datasheet** (verbatim, via B&C/Parts-Express 294-605): frequency range 1,000–18,000 Hz, 108.5 dB/W/m sensitivity, 60 W AES / 120 W program above the recommended 1.6 kHz crossover (12 dB slope), polyimide diaphragm, 44 mm aluminium voice coil.

### E. Is loudspeaker response minimum phase?
The canonical position (Atkinson/Stereophile; D'Appolito "Testing Loudspeakers"; ARTA/Bodziosoftware) is that a single driver's on-axis magnitude and phase are related by the Hilbert transform within its passband — i.e. minimum-phase — so magnitude EQ inverts it. The MLSSA/DAAS method (compute minimum phase via Hilbert transform, subtract from measured total phase to get **excess phase**, then excess group delay) is standard practice. Documented exceptions where response is NOT minimum-phase: multi-way summation through a crossover (ProSoundWeb: "Most multiway loudspeakers are non-minimum phase… excess phase shift due to the high order crossover networks"); ports/passive radiators; cabinet diffraction; driver acoustic-offset effects; and multi-source interference (the phase-plug case).

Reliability of Hilbert-derived excess phase in practice requires: (1) accurate removal of bulk propagation delay (the raw "excess phase" is dominated by an arbitrary time-of-flight term that must be estimated and subtracted — this ties directly to J); (2) good SNR; and (3) assumptions about response beyond the measured band (the ARTA paper notes response above the highest measured frequency must be assumed constant or extrapolated, and "errors are very large" if done naively). Failure modes: windowing/gating errors, LF truncation, and bulk-delay estimate ambiguity.

**Genuine disagreement to present honestly:** whether breakup resonances are minimum-phase. One camp holds each resonance, taken alone, is minimum-phase and thus magnitude-EQ-correctable. The other holds that a multi-modal radiating surface produces a far-field SUM of spatially distributed sources whose combination is non-minimum-phase (off-axis, and even on-axis near severe breakup). Both appear in the literature. The practical implication is the same regardless of which is right: EQ the smooth minimum-phase part, do not try to magnitude-EQ into a high-Q breakup peak.

### F. Dome/tweeter breakup by type
audioXpress (interviewing Jack Oclee-Brown of KEF, Laurence Dickie, and Erik Wiederholtz) states that for a 1" aluminium or titanium dome the first breakup mode "appear[s] around 20 kHz to 25 kHz" as an FR peak; stiffer materials (beryllium, diamond, thin-ply carbon) push it higher. Note KEF's own Uni-Q aluminium dome is engineered to ~40 kHz. Soft/fabric domes (ScanSpeak, SEAS, Morel, Peerless) break up more gently and lower but with self-damping, giving a broad, low-Q peak. Beryllium (Focal, SEAS Excel, Bliesma) and diamond push the first mode well up (diamond often >35–40 kHz), leaving the audio band cleaner. Ribbons/AMTs (Mundorf, Beyma, Adam) avoid classic dome breakup but have narrow vertical directivity.

**The metal-dome IMD claim is genuinely CONTESTED.** Some sources argue the sharp out-of-band breakup (~25–28 kHz) can be excited by ultrasonic content and fold IMD products into the audible band; others argue there is little musical energy there and the effect is negligible, while noting that manufacturers add phase shields/notch filters precisely to tame the peak. Real measured IMD data specifically isolating breakup-driven folding is thin — present as a disagreement, not settled fact. The software should MEASURE (multi-level test) rather than assume.

**Ribbon/AMT vertical directivity: CONFIRMED.** A tall thin radiating element gives wide horizontal but narrow vertical dispersion (measured examples cite ~15° vertical for tall ribbons; ESS AMT and Mundorf show similarly narrow vertical coverage), analogous to a line-source segment. This requires careful listening-height placement.

### G. Thermal headroom of a padded DE250
CONFIRMED that thermal is a weak objection at home levels. The DE250 handles 60 W AES (2-hour pink noise, crossover-to-20 kHz) and 120 W program. At home levels (~85–95 dB at the seat) from a 108.5 dB/W/m driver, the electrical input is on the order of milliwatts to tens of milliwatts even before the pad; with a ~25 dB pad the driver dissipates a tiny fraction of its rating, so power compression is remote. The ACTUAL dominant risks of HF boost are (1) exciting diaphragm/phase-plug resonances and breakup near the top, raising distortion; and (2) at the LOW end near horn cutoff, excursion and thus distortion/failure. Diaphragm failure from HF overdrive is typically fatigue/cracking of the thin diaphragm at resonance, not thermal burnout. Guidance: a modest top-octave shelf boost is acceptable where the response is minimum-phase; never boost near horn cutoff.

### H. Gain structure — analog vs digital attenuation
CONFIRMED. Standard gain-structure doctrine (Rane/Bohn; Biamp; Shure/McGregor) is to match DSP/DAC output to amplifier input sensitivity and set relative levels with the amplifier or a post-amp attenuator — not by dumping ~25 dB of digital attenuation upstream of a fixed-gain amp. The amp's input-referred noise is amplified the same regardless of the digital attenuation, but the horn's 108.5 dB/W/m sensitivity turns that noise into ~25 dB more audible hiss than the 83 dB woofer channel. Remedies and trade-offs: (a) lower-gain amplifier for the horn (best SNR); (b) L-pad/attenuator AFTER the amp (throws away power, adds series resistance, but preserves amp SNR); (c) input padding before the amp (simple, can load the source). A PCM5242-class DAC's high dynamic range (~112–114 dB) means the DAC is rarely the limit; the amp's input-referred noise usually dominates.

### I. Linearize each driver past the crossover first
CONFIRMED as established best practice. The target LR4 (or other) describes the desired ACOUSTIC slope, which is the product of the driver's natural response AND the applied electrical filter (Rane note 160: "the electrical response of the component network PLUS the raw acoustic response of the drivers themselves yield the result"). So you first flatten each driver through and beyond the overlap region, then apply the electrical filter that yields the intended acoustic LR shape (Linkwitz; VituixCAD/Kimmo Saunisto; DEQX, whose entire premise is driver linearization before crossover). The specific "linearize the woofer ~1 octave above and the tweeter ~1 octave below Fc" is a reasonable engineering rule of thumb — you need each driver well-behaved across the region where the opposite filter still passes meaningful energy, ~an octave for LR4 — but I found no canonical published constant fixing it at exactly one octave; treat it as sound-in-spirit folklore. Cut-only on the compression driver's LOW end is correct: below horn cutoff the horn loading collapses, excursion rises, and boosting there risks distortion and diaphragm damage.

### J. Delay / cycle-ambiguity
CONFIRMED. **Knapp & Carter, "The Generalized Correlation Method for Estimation of Time Delay," IEEE Trans. ASSP 24(4):320–327 (1976)** (DOI 10.1109/TASSP.1976.1162830) is the foundational GCC/GCC-PHAT reference. The matched-filter/time-bandwidth result gives cross-correlation peak width ∝ 1/bandwidth; the Cramér-Rao lower bound for delay variance scales inversely with bandwidth (and with SNR and observation time). So a narrow post-crossover overlap band (2–4 kHz) yields a broad, ambiguous correlation with a comb of near-equal side-peaks spaced ~1/Fc apart, whereas broadband LINEARIZED responses sharpen the main peak and remove ambiguity. Coarse-to-fine / multi-scale delay estimation (analogous to interferometric phase unwrapping and multi-frequency ranging) is documented in the DSP literature. In loudspeaker tools, REW, Acourate, VituixCAD, Smaart, ARTA and Klippel determine inter-driver delay/acoustic offset largely via impulse-response cross-correlation or minimum-phase/excess-phase alignment; the specific advice to "estimate delay on broadband linearized responses, then refine" is a well-founded synthesis you are making, arguably a modest novelty in this domain rather than a documented standard practice.

### K. In-room target slope
CONFIRMED that flat in-room steady-state is the wrong target. Flat anechoic on-axis is well-supported (Toole/Olive). But the preferred in-room steady-state response slopes DOWN above the transition region. Sean Olive (Head of Acoustic Research, Harman), in his 2009 "Subjective and Objective Evaluation of Room Correction Products," states: **"A flat in-room target response is clearly not the optimal target curve for room equalization. The preferred room corrections have a target response that has a smooth downward slope with increasing frequency. This tells us that listeners prefer a certain amount of natural room gain."** The proposed Harman target slope is ~**1 dB/octave** (≈10 dB across 20 Hz–20 kHz); rendered around a ~630 Hz pivot it runs roughly from +4.5 dB at 20 Hz to about −1 dB at 20 kHz, versus the shallower Brüel & Kjær 1974 house curve (~6 dB spread). Olive and Toole both note the optimal slope depends on loudspeaker directivity and room reflectivity. The SMPTE X-curve for cinema is a steeper contrasting case for large rooms. Critically for the software: gated quasi-anechoic per-driver verification should check against FLAT; steady-state in-room verification should check against a downward-sloping target. Verifying in-room flatness against flat would over-brighten the result.

### L. Directivity mismatch and ka
Arithmetic CONFIRMED. For a 5.5" driver the effective radiating diameter is ~4.4–4.5" (~112–115 mm), so a ≈ 56–57 mm. ka=1 when wavelength = circumference: f = c/(2πa) ≈ 343/(2π·0.057) ≈ **958 Hz**; ka=2 ≈ **1.9 kHz**. So near a 2 kHz crossover the cone is at ka≈2 — the onset of meaningful directivity/beaming, matching the "ka≈1 near 950 Hz, ka≈2 by 2 kHz" claim (ATC/Acoustic Frontiers cite the ka=2 guideline; "a 5" driver should be crossed at 1700 Hz or lower to respect ka=2"). The broader claim also holds: a beaming cone handing off to a horn opening into its wider controlled coverage creates a step in directivity index / power response that on-axis magnitude EQ cannot fix — and flattening on-axis can worsen the off-axis/power-response balance (Toole/Olive spinorama/CTA-2034-A; Geddes on directivity matching). Guidance: choose the crossover so the cone's beamwidth matches the horn's nominal coverage at Fc (e.g. match a 90° horn where the cone's beamwidth has narrowed to ~90°), which for a 5.5" cone + wide horn often implies a LOWER crossover than 2 kHz, or use a smaller midrange.

### M. Prior art for measurement-adaptive correction ceilings
DEQX (driver linearization before crossover), Trinnov, Acourate/Audiolense, Klippel QC/auralization, Hypex Filter Design and Danville dspNexus all do driver linearization and let the user or algorithm bound correction range. DEQX explicitly measures per-driver frequency/phase/group-delay and builds FIR correction, and its manual advises the optimum crossover "cannot be reliably determined from inspection of the frequency-response alone." However, I found no shipping DIY product that (i) uses measured EXCESS PHASE to decide where magnitude EQ is valid vs invalid, (ii) does closed-loop verification comparing ACHIEVED vs PREDICTED correction per band and backs off where they diverge, or (iii) runs multi-level distortion checks to detect bands where the transfer is nonlinear and therefore not linearizable. These three — especially combined and adaptive per driver-type — appear largely NOVEL in a DIY tool.

## Recommendations

**Overall staging.** Keep the four layers strictly ordered: (1) crossover commissioning from per-driver gated measurements, (2) per-driver linearization above/below Fc, (3) room correction, (4) user EQ. **Verify layers 1–2 against a FLAT gated quasi-anechoic target; verify layer 3 against a DOWNWARD-sloping in-room target (~1 dB/octave, directivity-aware).** This single change corrects the biggest latent error in the described workflow.

**Discover the ceiling, don't hardcode it.** For every driver, after measuring, compute the minimum-phase response (Hilbert transform of log-magnitude), remove bulk delay via broadband cross-correlation, and subtract to get excess phase / excess group delay. Where excess phase is small and smooth, magnitude EQ is invertible — allow correction. Where excess phase rises sharply (breakup, phase-plug interference, HOM region), cap or forbid boost. This operationalizes E, D, and M and is the novel, defensible core of the tool.

**Per driver class:**
- **Compression driver on horn (DE250):** Ceiling up to where excess phase stays low, typically ≤ ~12–14 kHz; above that expect phase-plug interference ripple — allow gentle shelving/cut, forbid narrow boosts into ripple. **Cut-only below ~1.3–1.6 kHz / near horn cutoff** (excursion). Thermal is a non-issue at home; hazards are breakup excitation and low-end excursion. Modest top-octave shelf boost OK if excess phase is clean.
- **Soft/fabric dome:** Broad low-Q breakup ~15–20 kHz; EQ gently to ~15 kHz, taper boost above. Low IMD risk.
- **Metal (Al/Ti) dome:** Sharp breakup ~20–28 kHz, out of band but excitable. **Cut-only or no correction above ~18 kHz;** never boost toward the peak. Run a two-level sweep (e.g., −10 dB and 0 dB), compare normalized responses / measure IMD to detect breakup-driven nonlinearity before allowing any top-octave boost.
- **Beryllium/diamond dome:** Breakup pushed high (Be ~30+ kHz, diamond >35–40 kHz); can EQ flat to ~20 kHz with low risk, but still cut-only into any residual peak.
- **Ribbon/AMT:** No classic dome breakup; linearize magnitude to spec limit, but flag **narrow vertical directivity** — require on-axis (ear-height) measurement and warn against crossing too low for the element size.

**Delay estimation (J).** Estimate inter-driver delay on the BROADBAND LINEARIZED responses, not the narrow overlap band, with coarse-to-fine refinement. Report correlation-peak sharpness / a confidence metric; if the peak is ambiguous (comb of near-equal optima), warn the user and fall back to polarity + reverse-null checks at Fc.

**Gain structure (H).** Recommend a lower-gain amp for the horn channel or a post-amp L-pad rather than large digital attenuation; surface the predicted noise penalty (≈ the pad value in dB referred to the horn's sensitivity) so the user sees the hiss trade-off before committing.

**Directivity (L).** Warn when the chosen crossover puts the cone above ka≈2 (compute from entered Sd/effective diameter). Recommend matching cone beamwidth to horn coverage at Fc; note that on-axis flatness alone cannot fix a DI step.

**Thresholds that would change these recommendations:** if a driver's measured excess phase stays flat and small up to 20 kHz (as with a well-behaved Be/diamond dome or a KEF-grade damped aluminium dome), raise its correction ceiling to 20 kHz; if a multi-level distortion test shows THD/IMD rising >~1–2 dB disproportionately when a band is boosted, back off the boost in that band; if the delay-estimation confidence metric is low, don't auto-apply delay — require user confirmation.

## Caveats and Genuine Disagreements / Data Gaps
- **Breakup minimum-phase (E/F):** Genuine disagreement — "individual resonance is minimum-phase" vs "multi-modal far-field sum is non-minimum-phase." Both in the literature. Practical guidance (EQ the smooth part, not the peak) is robust to which is right.
- **Metal-dome out-of-band IMD (F):** Contested and under-measured; isolated measurements of breakup-driven IMD folding into the audio band are scarce. Treat as plausible-but-unproven; measure rather than assume.
- **In-room target slope magnitude (K):** Studies converge on "downward" (~1 dB/octave per Harman; shallower for B&K 1974) but the exact slope depends on directivity and room. Don't hardcode one slope; make it directivity-aware and user-adjustable.
- **"One octave" linearization margin (I)** and **"estimate delay on broadband then refine" (J):** Sound engineering synthesis, not backed by a single canonical published constant/procedure — present as well-founded heuristics.
- **Adam diaphragm mass (B):** The "tens of milligrams" claim is wrong; it is 0.17 g (170 mg), confirmed from Adam's own spec sheets.
- **Neumann individual-calibration wording (C):** Neumann publishes ±0.5 dB unit tolerance and per-unit reproduction-accuracy stats but no explicit "each unit hand-aligned in an anechoic chamber" sentence — the tolerance implies rigorous per-unit measurement but is not phrased as individual calibration.
