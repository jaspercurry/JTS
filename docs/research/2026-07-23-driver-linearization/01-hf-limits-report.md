# High-Frequency Limits for Automated Loudspeaker Correction: Where to Stop Above ~8 kHz, and Why

> Verbatim owner-commissioned deep-research artifact (2026-07-23). See this
> directory's README for how it was adopted.

## TL;DR
- **Fit EQ only to ~8 kHz, verify to ~10 kHz, and merely observe/report from there to 20 kHz — for every microphone tier.** The dominant limit is not the driver but measurement trust: microphone directivity, aiming error, and calibration uncertainty all climb from sub-1 dB below ~8 kHz into the multi-dB range across 10–20 kHz, while the perceptual payoff of top-octave correction is small and vanishes with listener age. This holds even though the user's case is per-driver quasi-anechoic (not room) correction.
- **Cap boost hard near and above the ceiling.** Never boost into a compression driver's HF rolloff: the DE250's rolloff above ~16–18 kHz is a diaphragm/phase-plug resonance phenomenon that does not linearize with EQ and raises distortion and thermal risk. Shipping systems converge on ≤+6 dB dip-fill (Anthem) and default correction ceilings of 5 kHz (Anthem) to 500 Hz (most OEM Dirac); adopt 0 dB boost above the fit ceiling.
- **Sweep past the analysis band (to 22–24 kHz at 48 kHz fs) but analyze only to the ceiling.** Farina ESS best practice requires fade-in/out windows and headroom beyond the band of interest so deconvolution edge artifacts fall outside the analysis window; ending the sweep exactly at 18–20 kHz places pre/post-ring artifacts inside the band you care about.

## Key Findings

1. **Microphone trust collapses above ~8–10 kHz — this is the binding constraint.** A national-lab (PTB) free-field microphone calibration carries an expanded uncertainty (k=2) of ±0.2 dB to 5 kHz, ±0.3 dB at 6.3–10 kHz, but ±0.4 dB at 12.5–20 kHz. Manufacturing/unit-to-unit response tolerance for the B&K 4189 / GRAS 40AE class of 1/2" capsule is ±1 dB to ~8–10 kHz but widens to ±2 dB out to 20 kHz. Per Cirrus Research (citing IEC 61672-1), Class 1 sound-level-meter tolerance is ±1.1 dB at the 1 kHz reference but widens to +2.6/−3.6 dB at 10 kHz and +3.5/−17 dB at 16 kHz — the standards body itself declines to constrain the top octave. A 1/2" free-field mic's on-axis diffraction gain (NIST/Nedzelnitsky) reaches ~9 dB at 19–20 kHz, so 0°-vs-90° divergence and aiming sensitivity grow steeply above 10 kHz.

2. **The consumer UMIK-class calibration is only trustworthy to ~8–10 kHz in practice.** miniDSP's own UMIK-2 manual (§5.3) states the two supplied cal files (0° and 90°) "differ only above a few kHz, due to the high-frequency directionality of the microphone," and (§5) that for speaker work "at high frequencies (above 5 kHz) adjustment of EQ or target curve by ear is likely to be needed anyway"; §7.1.1 notes the cal file "only goes up to 20 kHz." Cross-Spectrum Labs states its UMIK cal files range 5 Hz–25 kHz and "compare favorably with the results generated by a NVLAP NIST-traceable laboratory," and that drift over four years should be "no more than 1 decibel or so." Independent comparison found factory miniDSP files within ~2 dB of an Earthworks M23 reference while CSL files were within ~0.5 dB — i.e., factory-file HF error alone can be ~2 dB. Multiple documented UMIK-2 units read 3–6 dB high above 10 kHz in far-field use.

3. **Positional/aiming repeatability is the killer at short wavelengths.** Wavelength is 34 mm at 10 kHz, 21 mm at 16 kHz, 17 mm at 20 kHz. A compression driver on a horn has steep off-axis rolloff, so a cm-scale mic move or a few-degrees aiming error produces multi-dB swings at 16–20 kHz. miniDSP explicitly advises that above 5 kHz correction by ear is likely needed anyway.

4. **Perceptual payoff of top-octave correction is small and age-dependent.** Toole & Olive's resonance-audibility work shows detection thresholds are lowest in the midrange and rise at frequency extremes, and are much lower (easier to detect) for wide-bandwidth low-Q spectral changes than for high-Q ones. ISO 7029 and extended-high-frequency audiometry show hearing loss begins ~18–35 years of age, is worse in men, and by age 50 many listeners cannot hear above ~16 kHz. Meyer & Moran (JAES 2007) found CD-band limiting inaudible; the literature is genuinely contested (Reiss meta-analysis, Oohashi "hypersonic effect") but even proponents locate any effect at the threshold of detectability.

5. **Never EQ up a compression-driver rolloff.** The B&C DE250 is specified 1–18 kHz, 108.5 dB sensitivity, 60 W AES / 120 W program, recommended crossover 1.6 kHz (44 mm aluminium voice coil, polyimide diaphragm). Its rolloff above ~16–18 kHz is a diaphragm/phase-plug/air-load resonance regime, not a minimum-phase electrical rolloff that boost can cleanly invert. Boosting there raises voice-coil heating, distortion, and offers no clean linearization. Anthem ARC caps dip-fill at +6 dB for speaker safety; Genelec's positive-gain feature is restricted by model; the pro-audio consensus (Linkwitz, ESP) is that boost is bounded by excursion/power headroom.

6. **Shipping prior art overwhelmingly limits HF correction — but mostly for room reasons.** Anthem ARC's default Maximum Correction Frequency is 5,000 Hz, with the explicit stated reason (ARC Genesis "Adjust Targets" documentation): *"For most systems, we recommend a setting of 5,000 Hz. Although this can be set as high as 20,000 Hz, we do not endorse such a large correction window. At higher frequencies, the microphone becomes directional thus affecting measurement accuracy."* Anthem's Nick Platsis put it directly (Audioholics interview): *"It is reliable up to 5 kHz. After that it starts becoming too directional with increasing frequency."* Most OEM Dirac ships limited-bandwidth (to 500 Hz). Audyssey builds in a deliberate HF rolloff ("Reference" curve) and a 2 kHz "midrange compensation" dip motivated by tweeter/midrange directivity change. Genelec/Neumann/Trinnov correct full-range in principle but with heavy HF smoothing and phase-focused (not aggressive magnitude) treatment. Crucially, most of these correct a ROOM response — where spatial averaging and reflections dominate — not a per-driver gated anechoic response.

## Details

### The core distinction: per-driver anechoic vs room correction
The user's system corrects a *driver's own quasi-anechoic on-axis response* at 1 m gated — fundamentally different from the room-correction prior art (Dirac, Audyssey, ARC, Trinnov, Genelec, Neumann). Room systems limit HF correction chiefly because (a) above the transition/Schroeder frequency the steady-state room response is dominated by direction-dependent reflections that spatial averaging cannot cleanly invert, and (b) listener head movement of a few cm decorrelates the top octave. Those specific reasons do **not** transfer to a gated anechoic per-driver measurement — the reflections are windowed out. However, two limits DO transfer and dominate the user's case: **microphone directivity/aiming/calibration uncertainty**, and **audibility of the correction target**. Anthem's own rationale for its 5 kHz default ("the microphone becomes directional thus affecting measurement accuracy") is precisely the mic-directivity limit, which is measurement-physics, not room-physics — so it applies to the user directly.

### Question 1 — Microphone calibration reliability 10–20 kHz
- **National-lab calibration uncertainty (PTB, primary):** ±0.2 dB (100 Hz–5 kHz), ±0.3 dB (6.3–10 kHz), ±0.4 dB (12.5–20 kHz), expanded (k=2). NIST/Nedzelnitsky free-field reciprocity: ~0.07 dB (5–20 kHz) for the primary method. Comparison/substitution methods (what consumer mics actually get): ±0.36–0.52 dB (k=2) across 125 Hz–20 kHz.
- **Unit-to-unit variance:** B&K 4189 / GRAS 40AE class published free-field response tolerance is ±1 dB to ~8–10 kHz, widening to ±2 dB to 20 kHz. This ±2 dB envelope is the effective unit-to-unit spread the individual cal file is meant to remove — but the *residual* after a comparison-grade cal file is the dominant real-world error.
- **Consumer/electret reality:** miniDSP factory files can be ~2 dB off an Earthworks reference above 10 kHz; CSL comparison-grade files ~0.5 dB. Electret capsules are harder to characterize at HF than externally-polarized lab standards. Documented UMIK-2 far-field HF errors of 3–6 dB exist (attributed to cal-file mismatch and self-noise, not necessarily typical, but illustrative of tail risk).
- **0° vs 90° and aiming error:** For a 1/2" capsule the 0°-vs-90° difference is ~3–5 dB by 10 kHz and ~7–9 dB by 20 kHz. Using the wrong cal file (0° vs 90°) therefore injects several dB of error in the top octave and near-zero below ~5 kHz. A ±30° aiming error at 20 kHz is on the order of ~1–3 dB; ±15° well under ~1 dB at 20 kHz and smaller at 16 kHz (inferred from monotonic polar behavior; no clean published ±15°/±30° table found — genuine data gap). Below ~8 kHz, aiming and cal error are each sub-1 dB.
- **IEC 61672-1 Class 1** widening to +3.5/−17 dB at 16 kHz and effectively unbounded negative at 20 kHz is the clearest standards-level admission that the top octave is not metrologically constrained for field instruments.

### Question 2 — Positional repeatability of gated 1 m measurements
Wavelengths: 34/21/17 mm at 10/16/20 kHz. At 16 kHz a half-wavelength is ~10 mm, so a 1 cm mic displacement can swing a comb-sensitive response by many dB. For a horn/compression driver with a steep off-axis rolloff, a few degrees of angular error translates to a directly proportional dB error at the top octave. The practical consequence: at 16–20 kHz, run-to-run and setup-to-setup variance easily exceeds the perceptual threshold, so top-octave data is good enough to *observe/report* but not to *fit* an EQ to. Klippel NFS / large-anechoic data reaches ~1% precision, but that is lab equipment; a single-point gated 1 m sweep with a consumer USB mic cannot approach it above 10 kHz. Multi-position averaging *helps* below the transition region but at HF it smears genuine on-axis detail (each position has different comb structure) — so for per-driver anechoic linearization, averaging positions HURTS top-octave fidelity; better to fix a repeatable single on-axis point and smooth.

### Question 3 — Audibility
- **Resonance detection (Toole & Olive 1988, JAES 36(3):122–142, e-lib id 5163):** thresholds are frequency-, Q-, and program-dependent; lowest (most sensitive) in the midrange, and detection is easier for low-Q, wideband-excited resonances than for high-Q ones. Best-case mid-band detection is reported around a few tenths of a dB with revealing signals; typically ~0.75–1 dB. Thresholds RISE at HF extremes.
- **Hearing loss (ISO 7029:2017; extended-HF audiometry):** age-related loss starts ~18–35 y, worse in men; the 20-year-old may hear to ~18–20 kHz, the 40-year-old typically to ~15–16 kHz, the 60-year-old often only to ~10–12 kHz. Correcting 16–20 kHz thus has essentially zero payoff for the 60-year-old and marginal payoff for the 40-year-old.
- **Top-octave / hi-res debate (flag as contested):** Meyer & Moran (JAES 2007, 55(9):775–779, e-lib id 14195) — CD-band A/D/A loop inaudible. Reiss (2016 meta-analysis) — trained listeners can detect a difference at low effect size. Oohashi et al. "hypersonic effect" — non-auditory EEG effects, disputed and not a sound-quality-rating effect. Music spectral energy above 16 kHz is typically very low. **Verdict: the perceptual payoff of correcting 16–20 kHz is small even for young listeners and negligible for typical (age 40+) listeners.**

### Question 4 — Driver protection and distortion
The B&C DE250 is rated 1–18 kHz, 108.5 dB sensitivity, 60 W AES / 120 W program, recommended crossover 1.6 kHz. Its HF rolloff is governed by diaphragm mass/breakup, phase-plug geometry, and air-load resonance — a regime where response is not minimum-phase and does not invert cleanly with boost. Boosting into it: (a) increases voice-coil dissipation and power compression; (b) can raise HD/IMD near the band edge; (c) chases a resonance that shifts with level. Pro-audio practice (Linkwitz, ESP Linkwitz-transform writings) frames all boost as bounded by excursion/power headroom. Shipping-system enforcement: Anthem ARC limits dip-fill to **+6 dB**; Dirac max boost ~10 dB (bass context); Genelec restricts positive gain by model. **Recommendation: 0 dB boost above the fit ceiling; cap boost to ≤+3–4 dB in the octave below the ceiling, and only to fill narrow dips, never to extend a rolloff.**

### Question 5 — Prior-art ceilings (documented vs inferred)
| System | Documented HF correction behavior | Stated rationale |
|---|---|---|
| **Anthem ARC / ARC Genesis** | Default Max Correction Frequency **5,000 Hz** (user-adjustable 400 Hz–20 kHz); +6 dB dip-fill cap | *Documented (verbatim):* "we do not endorse such a large correction window. At higher frequencies, the microphone becomes directional thus affecting measurement accuracy" |
| **Dirac Live** | Full-range 20 Hz–20 kHz on full license; most OEM ships **Limited Bandwidth to 500 Hz** | Mixed-phase correction; recommends 44.1/48 kHz measurement |
| **Audyssey MultEQ / XT32** | Deliberate HF rolloff on "Reference"; **2 kHz "midrange compensation" dip**; "Flat" removes rolloff | *Documented:* compensates tweeter/midrange directivity change at crossover |
| **Genelec GLM AutoCal / AutoCal 2** | Room compensation concentrated low/low-mid; full-range level/response with HF smoothing | Room-acoustic anomalies are low-frequency dominated |
| **Neumann MA 1** | 20 Hz–20 kHz alignment, Fraunhofer algorithms; heavy room-target HF smoothing | Room-specific target; mode compensation emphasis |
| **Trinnov Optimizer** | Full-range amplitude+phase; user limiter curve; IIR default from 150 Hz | Time/frequency; "knowing which defects may be corrected without creating additional problems" |
| **Sonos Trueplay** | Phone-mic, moving measurement; iOS-only for full tuning | *Documented:* cannot guarantee consistent microphone calibration across Android hardware |

The pattern: **room-correction systems limit or heavily smooth HF for room reasons; the one system that gives an explicit *measurement-physics* reason (Anthem: mic directivity) sets its default at 5 kHz.** That 5 kHz figure is the closest documented analogue to the user's constraint.

### Question 6 — Sweep design at 48 kHz
Farina's ESS method (AES 108th, 2000, paper 5093; AES 122nd, 2007, paper 7121) and Müller & Massarani require pre/post-equalization and **fade-in/fade-out windows**; deconvolution produces pre-ringing at band edges. At 48 kHz fs, Nyquist is 24 kHz, and the DAC reconstruction filter and mic anti-alias filter impose amplitude/phase distortion in the 20–24 kHz region. **Ending the sweep exactly at 18–20 kHz places the fade-out taper and edge artifacts inside the analysis band.** Best practice: sweep with headroom (e.g., 10 Hz–24 kHz or at least to ~22 kHz), apply proper fades, and analyze only up to the fit/verify ceiling — sweeping *past* the analysis band improves the analysis band by moving edge artifacts out of it. There is no measurement-quality reason to stop at 18 kHz specifically; that number is the driver spec, not a sweep-design optimum.

## Recommendations

### Recommended policy table
| Mic tier | Observe/report band | Fit (correction) ceiling | Verify ceiling | Smoothing vs frequency | Max boost near/above ceiling |
|---|---|---|---|---|---|
| **Reference (UMIK-2, per-serial cal)** | to 20 kHz | **8 kHz** (extend to 10 kHz only with fixed on-axis jig + 0° cal file confirmed) | **10 kHz** | 1/6-oct ≤1 kHz; 1/3-oct 1–8 kHz; 1/2–1-oct 8–20 kHz | ≤+4 dB in octave below ceiling (narrow dips only); **0 dB above ceiling** |
| **Consumer (Dayton iMM-6C class)** | to 16–20 kHz | **6 kHz** | **8 kHz** | 1/3-oct ≤1 kHz; 1/2-oct 1–6 kHz; 1-oct above | ≤+3 dB below ceiling; **0 dB above** |
| **Phone / built-in mic** | to ~10 kHz (report only; treat as indicative) | **3 kHz** (bass/lower-mid only) | **5 kHz** | 1/2-oct ≤500 Hz; 1-oct above | ≤+3 dB below ceiling; **0 dB above** |

**Justification of each ceiling:** the reference-tier 8 kHz fit ceiling is set by PTB calibration uncertainty crossing ~0.3 dB and unit tolerance widening past 8–10 kHz, plus Anthem's documented 5 kHz mic-directivity rationale (the user's per-driver case tolerates slightly higher because reflections are gated out, hence 8 kHz not 5 kHz); verify to 10 kHz because observation tolerates more error than fitting. Consumer tier drops to 6 kHz because factory electret cal files carry ~2 dB HF error. Phone tier drops to 3 kHz because Sonos itself will not trust phone-mic HF calibration across hardware. Boost caps follow Anthem's +6 dB safety precedent and the "never EQ a rolloff" pro-audio consensus, tightened for the compression driver's resonant HF rolloff.

### Staged next steps
1. **Ship the table above as default.** Set fit=8 kHz (reference), verify=10 kHz, boost=0 above ceiling immediately.
2. **Sweep 10 Hz–24 kHz with fade windows; analyze only to ceiling.** Fix the reconstruction/anti-alias edge issue first.
3. **Instrument repeatability.** Log run-to-run variance 8–20 kHz across ≥5 re-seatings per mic tier. **Threshold to change policy:** if reference-tier re-seating variance stays <1 dB to 12 kHz with a fixed jig, raise the reference fit ceiling to 10 kHz. If consumer variance >2 dB by 6 kHz, lower its ceiling to 5 kHz.
4. **Enforce cal-file/orientation checks.** Verify 0° file + on-axis aiming; reject sessions where the 90° file is loaded for on-axis work (multi-dB HF error).
5. **Never extend the DE250 rolloff.** Above ~14 kHz, cut-only.

## Caveats
- **Genuine literature disagreements (not averaged):** (a) Top-octave audibility — Meyer & Moran (inaudible) vs Reiss meta-analysis (detectable, small) vs Oohashi (non-auditory) genuinely conflict; I weight toward "small/negligible payoff for correction" but flag the dissent. (b) Whether to correct above 10 kHz at all — room-correction vendors split between full-range (Dirac/Genelec/Neumann/Trinnov) and hard-limited (Anthem 5 kHz default, OEM Dirac 500 Hz); the split is partly philosophical.
- **Thin/absent evidence:** No clean published dB-vs-angle table at ±15°/±30° for 16/20 kHz for 1/2" capsules (only graphical polar families); no UMIK-2-specific published directivity dataset; no published run-to-run variance study for gated 1 m consumer-mic sweeps above 10 kHz specifically. These are the honest data gaps — the recommendation is built on adjacent primary data (NIST diffraction, PTB uncertainty, IEC tolerances) plus first-principles wavelength arguments.
- **Per-driver vs room distinction shapes everything:** because the user gates out reflections, the *room* reasons for HF limits (spatial averaging, listener movement) do not apply, which is why the reference ceiling can sit at 8 kHz rather than the 500 Hz of OEM room systems. But the *measurement-physics* reasons (mic directivity, aiming, calibration) and *audibility* reasons fully apply and set the ceiling.
- Forum/enthusiast sources (miniDSP community, AVS, diyAudio) were used only where they reported measured data (e.g., UMIK factory-vs-CSL-vs-Earthworks comparison, documented 3–6 dB UMIK-2 HF errors) and are flagged as non-lab, single-sample observations, not authoritative.
