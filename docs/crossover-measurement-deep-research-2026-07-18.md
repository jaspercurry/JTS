## TL;DR

- **Replace the single-tone level lock with a driver-sensitivity + crest-factor model that predicts the sweep's peak dBFS before playing it, back it up with one low-level pilot sweep, then ACCEPT-AND-NORMALIZE hot-but-unclipped captures instead of reject-and-retry.** This alone eliminates most of the "too loud" iteration. Move the per-driver capture distance from 3 cm out to a more forgiving ~10–20 cm (still quasi-near-field for a 5.5" woofer and 1" horn at the ~2 kHz crossover), because at 3 cm a millimeter of hand movement is several dB and the tweeter's ~108 dB sensitivity trivially overloads a phone ADC.
- **Recover relative level, polarity, and delay from a SINGLE continuous recording containing BOTH drivers played back-to-back (sequential chirps on one ADC timeline).** The unknown, drifting playback→capture latency cancels in the *difference* of the two arrivals, so this is the correct primitive under the no-loopback constraint — but keep the two chirps close in time (target <0.4 s apart) because clock drift corrupts the relative delay by ≈ ppm × gap (50 ppm × 1 s = 50 µs ≈ 2.4 samples ≈ 17 mm of phantom path error at 48 kHz). Validate with a reverse-null / summed-flatness check at the crossover.
- **Trust magnitude-derived (minimum-phase) reasoning for the woofer and for EQ, but NOT for the compression-driver/horn tweeter near its cutoff, and never for the acoustic offset between drivers** — that offset is excess (non-minimum) phase and must be measured, not inferred. The honest limit on a phone mic is ~1 sample (≈20.8 µs / ≈7.15 mm) for integer peak-picking, improvable to sub-sample (~µs) with parabolic or phase-slope (GCC-PHAT-style) interpolation in good SNR.

## Key Findings

1. **The "too loud" problem is a leveling-reference error, not a distance error alone.** A single steady tone sets gain by one frequency's response; a full-band sweep's energy lands wherever the driver+DSP response peaks. For a horn tweeter with a rising/peaky response and a mandatory high-pass, the sweep's peak bin can sit well above the lock tone. The fix is to level against the *expected peak of the actual stimulus*, computed from the driver sensitivity model and the target filter, and confirmed with a quiet pilot sweep — not against one tone.
2. **A hot-but-not-clipped capture is fully valid.** Magnitude and phase are preserved under any known linear gain; only true ADC clipping (samples pinned at digital full scale) or analog front-end overload destroys information. Serious tools already lean this way: REW aborts a sweep only when *heavy* clipping occurs — its help states, "*Abort if heavy input clipping occurs will abort the measurement if more than 30% of the samples in an input block are clipped*" — and otherwise merely warns. The correct policy is accept-and-normalize with a modest guard band, reserving retry for genuine clip/overload.
3. **The no-loopback constraint is real and cannot be fully defeated — but relative timing survives it.** You cannot recover a driver's *absolute* acoustic delay from an isolated capture (unknown, variable, drifting latency). But if both drivers are excited within *one* recording, the common latency cancels in the arrival-time *difference*. This is exactly what REW's "acoustic timing reference" exploits, and what the DIY community does with dual-channel / semi-dual-channel VituixCAD workflows.
4. **Clock drift sets a hard, quantifiable accuracy budget.** Consumer crystals are typically ±20–100 ppm — Texas Instruments' crystal-selection app report (SLLA122) states, "*The recommended frequency stability or temperature tolerance of the crystal is ±50 ppm. The maximum acceptable frequency stability or temperature tolerance of the crystal is ±100 ppm.*" Drift error in the relative delay ≈ ppm × time-gap-between-chirps. Keep the two chirps <~0.4 s apart to hold error under one sample at 48 kHz. Long single sweeps on asynchronous clocks get convolved with an allpass "skew" filter (Bryan/Kolar/Abel 2010; Farina's "skewed IR"), and *averaging* repeated sweeps across drifting clocks causes comb filtering that is worse than a single sweep — Gamper (HSCMA 2017) notes that "*when averaging over repeated runs… clock drift causes temporal misalignment between successive frames.*" REW enforces this in practice: "*Do not use multiple sweeps if the input and output are on different devices (for example, if the input is a USB mic).*"
5. **Minimum-phase assumptions are a scalpel, not a hammer.** Individual drivers are largely minimum-phase in-band, so magnitude constrains phase and EQ can be planned from magnitude alone. But (a) the acoustic-center offset between woofer and tweeter is pure excess phase / delay and is invisible to minimum-phase math; (b) a compression driver on a horn departs from minimum-phase near its cutoff and where reflections/HOMs intrude; (c) reflections captured in the window make any response non-minimum-phase. So: minimum-phase for EQ and the woofer, measured phase/delay for alignment.

## Details

### 1. The measurement model (excite, capture, estimate, and resolve "too loud")

**Excitation.** Use the exponential (log) sine sweep with Farina deconvolution (inverse-filter / frequency-domain division) — the field standard because it (a) has a naturally pink spectrum, putting more energy at low frequencies where SNR is worst, (b) pushes harmonic-distortion products to negative time so they window out, and (c) has a low crest factor (~4 dB measured for the log-sine chirp vs ~11–12 dB for pink-filtered MLS, per the SRS/Ian Chan analysis), giving ~6–8 dB more usable level for the same peak headroom. The tweeter high-pass must be verifiably live in the compiled DSP profile before any excitation; the sweep start frequency for the tweeter should sit safely inside its protected band.

**The leveling strategy that works on the first try.** Layer three ideas:

- **Model-first (open-loop prediction).** You know each driver's sensitivity (woofer ~83 dB, tweeter ~108 dB — a 25 dB spread), the DSP gain, the target filter shape, and the mic sensitivity (UMIK-2: quoted as −34 dBFS @ 0 dB gain / 94 dB SPL, 125 dB max SPL; Dayton iMM-6C: ~−40 dBV/Pa capsule, ~120 dB SPL max for 1% THD). From these you can predict the SPL at the capsule and therefore the peak dBFS the sweep will produce, per driver, before playing it. Set gain so the *predicted peak* lands near the top of the clean window. This sensitivity-model + crest-budget approach is what lets you skip iteration.
- **Pilot sweep (closed-loop confirmation).** Play one short, quiet (e.g., −30 to −24 dBFS) full-band sweep through the protected driver, measure the *actual* peak bin, and compute the exact gain that puts that peak at target. Because a sweep excites every frequency, this captures the true worst-case bin a single tone misses. One pilot + one real capture beats N re-levels.
- **Accept-and-normalize.** If the real capture comes back hot but with no clipped samples and no front-end overload, keep it and normalize the known gain offset out. Only retry on true clipping.

**Window and dynamic lower bound.** REW's thresholds are instructive: its help states the headroom figure "*is red if there is less than 6 dB of headroom… green between 6 and 40 dB (or between 6 and 60 dB for USB mics)*," and it warns above that (wasted SNR). Its default sweep RMS level is −12 dBFS ("*The default value is −12 dBFS. This control is normally preset to the Sweep Level established during the Check Levels process*"). The current −45…−18 dBFS window is reasonable for the *lock point* but should be set dynamically: measure the ambient noise floor from the pre-sweep silence and require the stimulus to sit a target SNR (≥40 dB for FR, more for clean phase) above it, while keeping peaks a guard band (~6 dB) below full scale. In a quiet room, push level up for SNR; in a noisy room, the floor raises the lower bound and you accept less headroom.

**Distance as a variable.** Keele's near-field criterion — from his 1974 JAES paper, "to be within 1 dB of the true nearfield pressure, the microphone must be no farther from the center than 0.11× the diaphragm effective radius," with near-field validity up to f_max ≈ 4311/D (inches) — makes 3 cm defensible *acoustically* for the woofer's low end but hostile *ergonomically*: the SPL at 3 cm is enormous (the 108 dB tweeter slams a phone ADC) and position sensitivity is millimeters-per-dB. For a consumer flow, prescribe a more forgiving, repeatable distance (~10–20 cm on-axis) and *adapt target level to the measured distance and driver sensitivity*. You lose some room-immunity, which you recover by windowing the impulse response to exclude the first reflection (a 5.5" cabinet at 10–20 cm still gives a usable reflection-free window around the ~2 kHz crossover). Reserve true 3 cm near-field only for an optional "woofer low-frequency extension" splice, à la Keele/Struck-Temme, if you ever need sub-200 Hz accuracy.

**Mic specifics that change the flow.**

- **UMIK-2:** UAC2, 32-bit ADC, software-controllable analog gain (0–60 dB via miniDSP's "miniDSP-MIK" app, not exposed to the browser), per-serial cal file with a `Sens Factor` line. Because gain is set-and-forgotten in firmware, the browser flow should *assume a fixed known gain* and encode it in the level model; it cannot change UMIK-2 gain mid-flow. Sensitivity is quoted two ways across sources (−34 dBFS @ 0 dB gain vs −31.9 dBFS at default gain / 94 dB SPL), so read the actual cal-file `Sens Factor` rather than a spec number.
- **Dayton iMM-6C:** self-contained ADC/DAC (CM6542), ~10 mV/Pa capsule (about 6 dB less sensitive than the analog iMM-6 per user comparisons), ~120 dB SPL max for 1% THD, per-serial CSV cal file, no user gain control. Its lower sensitivity *helps* hot near-field tweeter captures (more acoustic headroom before overload) but *hurts* SNR on the quiet woofer — so the level model must branch on mic identity.
- **Arbitrary phone/USB mics:** the killer is browser `getUserMedia` DSP. Chrome/Firefox/Safari apply autoGainControl, echoCancellation, and noiseSuppression by default; AGC silently rewrites your levels and EC/NS destroy magnitude and phase. You MUST request `autoGainControl:false, echoCancellation:false, noiseSuppression:false`, and historically on Chrome AGC could only be defeated by also disabling echoCancellation. Verify via `MediaStreamTrack.getSettings()` that the constraints actually took, and refuse to measure (or warn hard) if they didn't. Uncalibrated phone mics have unknown sensitivity and rolled-off/peaky extremes; without a cal file, absolute SPL and HF magnitude are untrustworthy, though relative timing/polarity still work.

### 2. The crossover-alignment model (level, polarity, delay without a loopback)

**Physics recap.** At the crossover, summed pressure P(f) = W(f) + T(f) (complex). A flat sum requires level match, correct relative polarity, and time alignment (τ = Δd/c; phase error = 2πfτ). For an LR4 target both sections are −6 dB at Fc and in-phase, summing flat; wrong delay tilts the vertical lobe and digs a notch; wrong polarity turns the intended flat sum into a deep null (or, for even-order filters, the reverse-null test deliberately *produces* that null to prove alignment).

**The right primitive: single-capture, both-drivers.** Play the woofer chirp then the tweeter chirp (or a phase-reference chirp then each driver) inside one continuous recording. Deconvolve each to its own impulse response on the *shared* ADC timeline. The relative delay is the difference of IR peak positions (or the cross-correlation lag). Because both events share the one unknown latency L, the difference (arrival_B − arrival_A) = T_gap + (prop_B − prop_A) — **L cancels exactly**. This is the only approach inherently immune to the unknown, drifting absolute latency, and it maps directly onto REW's acoustic-timing-reference concept (one speaker's HF chirp anchors time for the others' sweeps, all in one session with the reference present every time).

**Failure modes and how to defend:**

- *Clock drift across the gap.* Error ≈ ppm × T_gap. Worked example at 48 kHz (20.833 µs/sample; sound travels 7.15 mm/sample at 343 m/s): 50 ppm × 1 s = 50 µs ≈ 2.4 samples ≈ 17 mm of phantom path error; a 2 s gap doubles it; 20 ppm × 1 s ≈ 0.96 samples. To keep the error under one sample, require T_gap < 20.8 µs / (ppm×10⁻⁶) → <0.42 s at 50 ppm, <1.04 s at 20 ppm. Mitigation: keep the two chirps as close together as possible, and optionally estimate the drift ratio from the recording itself (Gamper's blind method: play/record the excitation ≥ twice and least-squares-fit the resampling ratio; Microsoft's MATLAB code, `microsoft/Asynchronous_impulse_response_measurement`, is public). Do NOT time-average multi-second sweeps across the phone clock.
- *IR peak-picking on a horn tweeter.* The compression driver/horn is not cleanly minimum-phase near cutoff; its IR peak can be broad or double-humped. Prefer cross-correlation of the two drivers over the crossover band (band-limited to ~Fc/2…2Fc) plus parabolic or phase-slope (GCC-PHAT-style) sub-sample interpolation. Integer peak-pick at 48 kHz is limited to ~20.8 µs / 7.15 mm; interpolation buys sub-sample (~µs) precision in good SNR (Bryan/Abel report ≈0.002-sample precision with quadratic peak interpolation in a clean loopback; Céspedes et al. report parabolic-fit worst-case ≈1.25% of 1/f₀, improved to <0.5% by bias-compensated/matched-filter variants).
- *Near-field phase distortion.* At 3 cm the path-length difference to the two drivers is geometry-dominated and mic-position-sensitive; alignment is better done at the more forgiving prescribed distance on the design axis.

**Second, corroborating primitive: summed-response optimization.** With a combined measurement near Fc, search over {polarity, delay} for the flattest sum / shallowest notch (or, for even-order, the deepest reverse-null when one driver is inverted). Robust and self-validating but ambiguous in narrow bands (several delays can flatten one octave), so use it to *refine and validate* the single-capture delay estimate, not as the sole source. The reverse-null test is the DIY gold standard for even-order crossovers and is easy to show the user as a big dip that fills in when alignment is correct.

**Geometry assist.** A user-attested or system-inferred woofer–tweeter path difference gives a coarse τ that (a) seeds the search, (b) sanity-checks the measured delay (reject physically impossible values), and (c) resolves wrap ambiguity in phase-slope estimation. Geometry alone is too coarse for final alignment (acoustic centers sit inside the drivers at frequency-dependent depths), but it's an excellent prior — this is the poor-man's substitute for Trinnov's tetrahedral-mic triangulation.

**Minimum-phase: where it helps and hurts.** Use minimum-phase reconstruction to plan driver EQ (magnitude → phase for free) and to derive the woofer's in-band phase. Use *excess* group delay (measured minus minimum-phase) to extract the acoustic offset — exactly the D'Appolito / VituixCAD excess-group-delay method. Do not assume the horn tweeter is minimum-phase near its cutoff, and do not try to infer the inter-driver delay from magnitude — it is fundamentally excess phase.

**Estimator → outputs.** From the single capture, compute: relative level trim (band-averaged magnitude difference over the overlap band), polarity (sign that flattens the sum / deepens the reverse-null), delay (cross-correlation + sub-sample interpolation, drift-sanity-checked, geometry-bounded), and any residual EQ (minimum-phase driver linearization to hit the target acoustic slopes). The validator is the summed response: crossover-region ripple within tolerance (e.g., ±1.5 dB through Fc) and a clean reverse-null, shown to the user as a simple pass/fail with the before/after curve.

### 3. UX + logic per step (fewer taps, shorter time)

- **A. Declare drivers + target.** Keep, but pre-fill from a device profile (the reference hardware is known: 5.5"/83 dB woofer, 1"/108 dB horn, ~2 kHz LR4). The user confirms rather than researches. Bounds safe excitation and seeds the level model. *Taps saved: most of them.*
- **B. Protected setup by ear.** Keep as a fast safety gate (prove output→driver map and live tweeter high-pass) but auto-sequence it: one tap starts a scripted quiet tone-per-driver + combined check with automatic pass/fail, rather than manual per-tone taps.
- **C. Mic + level.** Replace the ramp-until-in-window loop with model-predicted gain + one pilot sweep + accept-and-normalize. Prescribe ~10–20 cm, not 3 cm. Auto-detect mic identity (UMIK-2 vs iMM-6C vs generic) and load the cal/sensitivity accordingly; verify getUserMedia constraints. *The single biggest time saver.*
- **D. Measure each driver.** Auto-run the stationary repeats (no per-repeat tap); use the "rule of two" correlation check between repeats to auto-reject transient-corrupted captures instead of asking the user. Do NOT average across drifting clocks — take the best single clean sweep or drift-correct before combining. Window out reflections automatically.
- **E. Align.** This becomes the new single-capture-both-drivers step: one recording, one position, on the design axis, at the prescribed distance. Compute level/polarity/delay automatically; validate with the reverse-null/summed-flatness check and show a one-glance pass/fail.
- **F. Apply.** Keep atomic compile-and-load.
- **G. Room correction (later).** Far-field at listening position on the applied active profile; this is where multi-position and the 90° cal file (for UMIK-2) come in.

Net: the flow collapses from "declare → ear-check → level-loop → per-driver-sweep-with-manual-repeats → separate combined alignment" into "confirm profile → one-tap safety gate → one-tap per-driver capture (auto-leveled, auto-repeated) → one-tap alignment capture (both drivers) → apply." Fewer captures, far fewer retries, most taps removed.

### 4. Prior art (what transfers, what doesn't)

- **REW:** Acoustic timing reference is the direct precedent for single-capture relative timing without loopback — one speaker's HF chirp anchors time for all sweeps; REW explicitly warns USB mics can't do loopback and that long sweeps on mismatched clocks distort the IR/phase (offering "Adjust clock with acoustic ref"). Default sweep level −12 dBFS; abort on >30% clipped samples; headroom 6–60 dB green for USB mics; multiple-sweep averaging disabled for USB mics. All of this transfers. What doesn't: REW assumes the mic and playback share a host or that the user manages the timing-reference speaker; your relay/E2E architecture must automate that.
- **Dirac Live / DLBC:** Measures each speaker with a sweep, uses an extra sweep on the first speaker for timing (Dirac's docs: "all impulse (phase/timing) fixes performed are based on the first signal sweep"), derives crossovers from the sweeps, and corrects level/delay/phase (FIR). DLBC's joint phase/delay/level bass optimization is the conceptual model for your summed-response validator.
- **Sonarworks SoundID / Audyssey MultEQ:** Consumer-grade guided multi-position sweeps; Audyssey uses low-level pulses for distance/level and derives speaker distance from time-of-flight (sometimes "wrong" in feet but right for delay). Proves a non-expert flow can auto-set delay/level — but both rely on a single host clock (mic into the same processor), which you don't have.
- **miniDSP:** UMIK + REW acoustic-timing app notes are essentially your reference implementation for USB-mic timing.
- **Trinnov Optimizer:** Uses a 4-capsule 3D mic to triangulate speaker position and auto-set level+delay+phase, with 2/3/4-way active crossovers. Lesson: geometry (their mic array) massively simplifies alignment. You can't ship a tetrahedral mic, so you substitute the single-capture-both-drivers timing trick + optional user geometry.
- **Klippel:** Near-field scanner and AN-series (near/far merge) are the rigorous version of Keele; confirms complex (magnitude+phase) summation of driver near-fields and the 0.11r rule. Lab-only, but validates your near-field-per-driver + windowing approach.
- **DIY / VituixCAD / ARTA:** Dual-channel or semi-dual-channel with a timing reference is the community standard; VituixCAD's excess-group-delay and "measure tweeter, woofer, and both in parallel" method is exactly your alignment estimator. Farina swept-sine and Majdak et al.'s multiple/interleaved exponential sweep method (MESM, JAES 2007) are the science for simultaneous multi-driver capture (though interleaving on independent clocks reintroduces drift risk, so sequential-close-together is safer for you).
- **Asynchronous-clock literature:** Bryan/Kolar/Abel, "Impulse Response Measurements in the Presence of Clock Drift" (129th AES Convention, 2010, Paper 8169) and Gamper, "Clock Drift Estimation and Compensation for Asynchronous Impulse Response Measurements" (HSCMA 2017, public MATLAB) are the citable core for clock-drift-robust deconvolution. Bryan et al.'s central result: an uncompensated asynchronous sweep yields an IR "convolved with an allpass filter having the same frequency trajectory form as the input swept sinusoid."

### 5. Explicit tradeoff resolution (Section 6)

- **Near-field vs far-field for driver measurement:** Two-tier. Prescribed ~10–20 cm quasi-near-field per driver for raw response (windowed), with optional true 3 cm splice only for deep-bass extension. Alignment at the same prescribed distance on the design axis. *Rationale: robustness and repeatability for a shaky hand beat textbook near-field purity you can't exploit on a phone.*
- **Leveling reference:** Sensitivity-model + crest budget as the predictor, pilot sweep as the confirmer. Single tone retired. *Rationale: only a full-band probe sees the sweep's true peak bin.*
- **Reject vs accept hot captures:** Accept-and-normalize; retry only on true clipping/overload. *Rationale: linear gain preserves magnitude and phase.*
- **Fixed vs adaptive distance/level:** Fixed prescribed distance, adaptive level (to measured distance, driver sensitivity, mic identity, ambient floor). *Rationale: a fixed distance is a simple, repeatable instruction; level must adapt or you're back to iterating.*
- **Relative phase/delay method:** Single-capture-both-drivers as primary; summed-response optimization + reverse-null as validator/refiner; geometry as prior/sanity-check. *Rationale: only single-capture is latency-immune; the others disambiguate and prove it.*
- **Magnitude-only vs magnitude+phase:** Magnitude+phase, with minimum-phase used only where valid (driver EQ, woofer) and measured excess phase for the inter-driver offset; treat horn-tweeter phase near cutoff as measured, not assumed. *Rationale: the whole point of alignment is excess phase.*
- **UX cadence:** Auto-run stationary repeats with automatic clean-capture selection (rule-of-two), minimal taps, hide the DSP. One alignment position for the crossover (add positions only in the later room pass). *Rationale: fewer actions, shorter time, non-expert-safe.*

**Advanced escape hatches** (behind an "expert" toggle): manual distance entry, manual gain override, raw magnitude/phase curves, choice of sweep length, manual polarity/delay nudge with live summed-response, and export of the impulse responses for VituixCAD/REW.

### 6. Staged adaptation plan (against A–G), ordered by leverage

**Stage 1 — small, high-leverage changes (ship first):**

1. Retire single-tone lock; implement sensitivity-model + crest-budget predicted gain (Step C). *Kills most "too loud" retries.*
2. Add the low-level pilot sweep confirmation (Step C).
3. Switch to accept-and-normalize for hot-but-unclipped captures (Steps C/D).
4. Move prescribed distance to ~10–20 cm; make target level adaptive to distance/sensitivity/mic/floor (Step C).
5. Enforce and verify `getUserMedia` constraints (autoGainControl/echoCancellation/noiseSuppression off) at session start (Step C).
6. Auto-run stationary repeats with rule-of-two clean-capture selection; stop averaging across drifting clocks (Step D).
7. Pre-fill driver/target profile for known hardware (Step A).

**Stage 2 — genuinely new subsystems:**
8. Single-capture-both-drivers relative-timing alignment: sequential close-spaced chirps in one recording, deconvolve to two IRs, cross-correlate with sub-sample interpolation, drift-sanity-check, geometry prior (replaces Step E).
9. Summed-response validator + reverse-null pass/fail with before/after display (Steps E/F).
10. Mic-identity auto-detection and per-mic level/cal branching (UMIK-2 vs iMM-6C vs generic) (Step C).

**Stage 3 — robustness/polish:**
11. Blind clock-drift-ratio estimation from the recording (Gamper method) to relax the chirp-gap constraint and improve phase fidelity.
12. Optional near-field bass-extension splice (Keele/Struck-Temme) for full-range accuracy.
13. Room-correction pass with multi-position and 90° cal handling (Step G).

Through-line: Stage 1 makes the flow *shorter and more reliable* with mostly parameter/logic changes; Stage 2 is where the hard no-loopback alignment problem gets its proper latency-immune solution; Stage 3 hardens it against clock drift and extends bandwidth.

## Recommendations

1. **Implement Stage 1 immediately** — it removes the dominant source of user friction (the re-level loop) with no new signal-processing subsystems: a better level model, a pilot sweep, accept-and-normalize, a saner distance, and mandatory getUserMedia constraint enforcement. **Benchmark to move on:** median captures-per-driver ≤ 1.2 and zero silent AGC corruption across a test matrix of ≥5 phones.
2. **Then build the single-capture-both-drivers aligner (Stage 2)** as the core IP. **Benchmark:** relative-delay repeatability across 10 sessions within ±1 sample (±20.8 µs) and summed crossover ripple ≤ ±1.5 dB through Fc with a visible reverse-null.
3. **Constrain the chirp gap to <0.4 s** until blind drift estimation (Stage 3) is in; document that this is a clock-drift budget decision (50 ppm × 0.4 s ≈ 1 sample at 48 kHz), not an arbitrary constant.
4. **Branch the level model on mic identity** and refuse to proceed on uncalibrated mics without warning that absolute SPL and HF magnitude are approximate (relative timing still valid).
5. **Show the user exactly one proof per hard step:** a filled-in reverse-null / flat summed response for alignment, and a green "in window, X dB SNR" for level. Hide everything else behind an expert toggle.
6. **Thresholds that change the plan:** if measured session-to-session drift routinely exceeds ~50 ppm (>1 sample error at your chosen gap), prioritize Stage 3 blind drift correction; if phone-mic SNR at the prescribed distance falls below ~40 dB in target rooms, either shorten distance or raise level within the guard band before considering longer sweeps.

## Caveats

- **Hard physical limit:** on a no-loopback phone mic you cannot recover *absolute* acoustic delay; you can only recover *relative* delay between drivers captured together. Absolute time-of-flight (and thus absolute distance) is unknowable per session. This is acceptable because crossover alignment only needs the relative term.
- **Clock-drift figures** (±20–100 ppm) are crystal-datasheet tolerances (TI SLLA122) plus one professional-interface measurement (~16–33 ppm from Bryan et al., i.e. ~1–2 ms/minute); no phone-specific ADC-clock ppm study was found, and the cheapest devices using ceramic resonators can be far worse (±0.5% = ±5000 ppm). Real phones may be better or worse; treat the <0.4 s gap as conservative until you measure your own device population.
- **The "two sequential chirps share one latency" cancellation** is a first-principles derivation consistent with the Bryan/Gamper drift model, not a directly published result for this exact scenario — validate empirically.
- **Minimum-phase validity for the horn tweeter near cutoff is genuinely uncertain;** measured phase should govern there.
- **Near-field at the relaxed 10–20 cm distance** trades some room immunity for ergonomics; the reflection-free window shrinks, so at very low frequencies the per-driver magnitude is less trustworthy than true 3 cm near-field — hence the optional bass-extension splice.
- **UMIK-2 sensitivity is quoted two ways** across sources (−34 dBFS @ 0 dB gain vs −31.9 dBFS at default gain / 94 dB SPL), and its analog gain is software-set; the flow must read the actual cal-file `Sens Factor` rather than assume a spec number.
- **Browser AGC/EC/NS behavior is inconsistent** across Chrome/Firefox/Safari and versions; constraint *requests* don't guarantee *effect*, so runtime verification via `getSettings()` is mandatory, not optional.
- **Sub-sample interpolation carries a known position-dependent bias** (parabolic fitting especially); Gaussian, sinc, matched-filter, or phase-slope (GCC-PHAT) interpolation is preferred at low SNR / higher reverberation.
